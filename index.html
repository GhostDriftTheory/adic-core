An Existence Theorem for
Constructive Interval Verification Systems

Mathematical Foundations of ADIC and Reduction to $\Sigma_1$ Formulas
GhostDrift Mathematical Institute
Abstract. In this paper, we establish the mathematical foundations of the Analytically Derived Interval Computation (ADIC) system. Specifically, we prove that for a natural class of real-valued computation programs, their analytic properties—specifically the satisfaction of inequality specifications—are logically equivalent to the truth of $\Sigma_1$ sentences in first-order arithmetic (e.g., PRA). This result reduces the verification of continuous real-number computations to the problem of the existence of finite, discrete certificates, herein referred to as ledgers.

1. Introduction

Ensuring the correctness of real-valued computation is a central challenge in computable analysis. Building upon the Type-2 Theory of Effectivity (TTE) by Weihrauch et al. and the constructive analysis of Bishop and Bridges, this paper reduces the verification process of real computation to a $\Sigma_1$ formula in formal arithmetic: "There exists a finite witness, verifiable by a primitive recursive function."

Theorem 1.1 (Main Theorem)
Let $\mathcal{O}$ be a family of basic functions satisfying standard assumptions of computable analysis (Definition 2.1). Then, for any program $P$ over $\mathcal{O}$, a halting input $x$, and an analytic specification $M(P,x)$, the following equivalence holds within the framework of first-order arithmetic (PRA or PA): $$ M(P,x) \iff \exists L \in \N \;.\; \Verify(P,x,L) = 1 $$ where $M(P,x)$ is a fixed analytic specification regarding the output value $v_{\mathrm{out}}$, and $\Verify$ is a primitive recursive predicate that validates the certificate $L$ against the specification.

1.1 Related Work and Positioning

The framework presented in this paper aligns with standard theoretical models, refining them into a computationally executable format:

  • Computability of Basic Functions: Beyond the classical framework of Weihrauch [1], our formulation is isomorphic to that found in the Handbook of Computability and Complexity in Analysis [3], the standard reference for computable analysis in the 2020s.
  • Constructive Analysis and Interval Extensions: Our approach is grounded in the classic work of Bishop and Bridges [2], as well as recent reconstructions in the Handbook of Constructive Mathematics [4] and Constructive Reverse Mathematics [11].
  • Arithmetization of Verification: Analogous to recent developments in the semantics [6] and program extraction [5] of Exact Real Computation, as well as formalizations in Coq [7], we reduce verification to operations on discrete structures.

The "ADIC Existence Theorem" proposed herein can be viewed as a reformulation of these standard theorems into an executable $\Sigma_1$ form. Furthermore, in the field of Rigorous Numerics, the practice of utilizing numerical computation logs as existence proofs is well-established [8, 9, 10]. ADIC provides a minimal existence theorem that unifies these practices through the concept of finite ledgers within a formal system.

2. Preliminaries and Definitions

Definition 2.1 (Family of Basic Functions)
Let $\mathcal{O} = \{ \op_j : \R^{k_j} \to \R \}_{j\in J}$ be a family of basic functions. We assume each $\op_j$ has a domain $D_j \subseteq \R^{k_j}$ and satisfies the following conditions (equivalent to the standard definition by Weihrauch [1]):
  1. $\op_j$ is defined on $D_j$ and is continuous on any bounded subset thereof.
  2. $\op_j$ admits an effective modulus of continuity $\omega$ (i.e., the required input precision is computable).
  3. The supremum and infimum of the range of $\op_j$ on bounded domains are effectively computable.

The domain $D_j$ of a basic function $\op_j$ is assumed to be a semi-algebraic set, and the membership problem $X_1\times\cdots\times X_{k_j}\subset D_j$ is decidable primitively recursively within PRA.

Definition 2.2 ($\mathcal{O}$-Program)
An $\mathcal{O}$-program is defined as a computation tree (or DAG) with rational constants/inputs as leaves and elements of $\mathcal{O}$ as internal nodes. For general programs involving loops, we consider the finite tree $\T_{P,x}$, obtained by unrolling the execution trace for a halting input $x$, as its canonical representation. The semantics $\denote{P}(x)$ is defined via the composition of functions $\op_j$ associated with each node.
The tree $\T_{P,x}$ can be encoded as a finite sequence, and its traversal is definable as a primitive recursive function.

3. Construction of Interval Extensions

Definition 3.1 (Local Soundness)
An interval operation $I_{\op_j} : \I^{k_j} \to \I$ is said to be locally sound if for any input interval vector $X \subseteq D_j$, the containment $$ \op_j(X) \subseteq I_{\op_j}(X) $$ holds.
Lemma 3.2
For any basic function $\op_j$ satisfying Definition 2.1, there exists a locally sound and computable interval extension $I_{\op_j}$, the construction of which is primitively recursively definable within PRA.
Proof (1) Arithmetic Operations: Standard interval operations, such as $I_+([a,b],[c,d]) := [a+c, b+d]$, suffice. Division is restricted to cases where the denominator interval does not contain $0$.

(2) Representative Example $\log$: Consider $\log$ on an interval $[a,b] \subset (0,\infty)$. Using the effective modulus $\omega$, we determine a partition width $\delta$ corresponding to a target error $\varepsilon$. We partition $[a,b]$ into sub-intervals of width at most $\delta$, and compute an approximate value $q_i$ at a representative point $c_i$ for each sub-interval (e.g., via Taylor expansion). Since the image is contained in $\bigcup [q_i - \varepsilon, q_i + \varepsilon]$, an enclosing interval $[L^-, L^+]$ can be constructed by taking the minimum and maximum of the bounds. This procedure reduces to a finite number of rational operations and comparisons.

(3) General $\op_j$: Given the assumptions in Definition 2.1 (uniform continuity and computability of range bounds), a piecewise approximation method analogous to that for $\log$ is applicable. $\square$

4. Arithmetization of Verification and Proof of Main Theorem

Definition 4.1 (Ledger and $\Verify$)
A Ledger $L$ is a finite sequence assigning an interval $X_n$ to each node $n$ of the computation tree $\T_{P,x}$. The predicate $\Verify(P,x,L)$ is a primitive recursive function that checks the following conditions:
  1. $L$ is isomorphic to the computation tree $\T_{P,x}$.
  2. Local soundness $\op(X_{child}) \subseteq X_{parent}$ holds at every node.
  3. The output interval $X_{\mathrm{out}}$ satisfies the specification $M(P,x)$.
Example 4.3 (Bounded Error Approximation of $\log$)
As a concrete example of a specification $M$, consider the proposition "Approximate $\log(q)$ within error $\varepsilon$": $$ M_{\log}(P, (q,\varepsilon)) \iff |\denote{P}(q,\varepsilon) - \log(q)| \le \varepsilon $$ In the ADIC framework, this is verified by checking that the output interval $X_{\mathrm{out}}$ is contained in $[\log(q)-\varepsilon, \log(q)+\varepsilon]$.
Theorem 4.2 (Restatement of Main Theorem)
For a halting $\mathcal{O}$-program $P$ and input $x$, the following logical equivalence holds: $$ M(P,x) \iff \exists L \in \N \;.\; \Verify(P,x,L) = 1 $$
Proof (Soundness $\Leftarrow$): Assume $\Verify(P,x,L)=1$. By Definition 4.1, local soundness is preserved at every step. By induction on the tree structure, the true value $v_{\mathrm{out}}$ of the root node is contained in the interval $X_{\mathrm{out}}$ (Global Soundness). Since $\Verify$ confirms that $X_{\mathrm{out}}$ satisfies the specification, it follows that the true value $v_{\mathrm{out}}$ also satisfies the specification.

(Realizability $\Rightarrow$): Assume $M(P,x)$ is true. Using the interval extension from Lemma 3.2, we construct a procedure $\Ledger$ that propagates intervals from the leaves to the root of the computation tree. By the principles of constructive analysis, the width of the output interval $X_{\mathrm{out}}$ can be made arbitrarily small by increasing the computation precision (e.g., the partition width). Since $M(P,x)$ is true, there exists sufficient precision such that $X_{\mathrm{out}}$ falls within the allowable range of the specification. The ledger $L$ generated at this precision satisfies $\Verify(P,x,L)=1$. $\square$

5. Conclusion

The foregoing discussion establishes the mathematical validity of the ADIC system. We have shown that the correctness of analytic real number computation is equivalent to the existence of finite certificates (ledgers) definable in first-order arithmetic. This provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for computer-assisted proofs of advanced mathematical objects, such as the verification of zeros of the $\zeta$ function.

Appendix A. Naive Construction of Interval Extension for $\log$

We detail a naive construction of the interval extension for the logarithm function.

Proposition A.1
Let $\log$ be equipped with an effective modulus $\omega$ and a rational point approximation procedure. Then, for any bounded interval $[a,b]$, a rational interval $I_{\log}([a,b])$ enclosing the range of $\log$ on $[a,b]$ can be constructively defined via a finite number of rational operations.
Proof Fix an error tolerance $\varepsilon>0$ and determine $\delta = \omega(\varepsilon/2)$. Partition the interval $[a,b]$ into $N$ segments using division points $u_i$ such that the width of each segment is at most $\delta$. Calculate an approximate value $q(c_i)$ at a representative point $c_i$ of each sub-interval with sufficient precision (error $\le \varepsilon/4$).
From the modulus of continuity, for any $x \in [u_{i-1}, u_i]$, we have $|\log x - \log c_i| < \varepsilon/2$. By the triangle inequality, $|\log x - q(c_i)| < \varepsilon$, implying $\log x \in [q(c_i)-\varepsilon, q(c_i)+\varepsilon]$. Consequently, an enclosing interval for the entire range is given by: $$ L^- := \min_i (q(c_i)-\varepsilon), \quad L^+ := \max_i (q(c_i)+\varepsilon) $$ Since these steps consist solely of a finite number of rational operations, the construction is primitive recursive. $\square$
